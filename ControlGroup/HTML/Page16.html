This study is being conducted as part of the Master’s thesis of Nadiya Davydova with the title "Implementation and Evaluation of Response Time and Performance Based Interventions in Intelligent Tutoring Systems" at the Department of Computational Methods in Modeling and Analysis of Learning Processes and investigates the impact of pedagogical interventions based on individual processing time in Intelligent Tutoring Systems. This study builds on the hypothesis that there is no linear relationship between step duration and correctness. On the one hand, a student needs a minimum amount of time in order to process the problem, retrieve appropriate information, and to construct a correct response. On the other hand, taking too long to carry out a step could indicate a lack of background knowledge, failure to retrieve critical information, and inability to address the step. In this study, we wanted to investigate, if certain pedagogical interventions based on the individual average step duration could minimize the overall error rate of students. In the experimental group, we measured individual average step duration and correctness of the first five tasks. For the other tasks, we then introduced two pedagogical interventions:

1. We enabled submission of solutions only after the first individually computed time threshold. We assume that the probability of the solution being incorrect before that time threshold is high because it implies that the student didn’t spend the minimum amount of time necessary for processing the problem and constructing a thought-out response. Therefore, our first intervention should counteract this by forcing participants to spend an appropriate amount of time working on a task.

2. We provided a hint to participants who didn’t submit their solution after the second individually computed time threshold. We assume that the probability of the solution being incorrect after that time threshold is high because it implies that the student struggles in figuring out the correct solution. Therefore, our second intervention should counteract this by pushing the participant in the correct direction with a hint.
